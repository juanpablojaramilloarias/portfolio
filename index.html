How we went from napkin sketches to 1,300 users and an acquisition in 18 months — the product decisions, real artifacts, and lessons from building for Colombia's restaurant industry.

Cronos product walkthrough — shift scheduling, payroll, and team management in action.

Chapter 1

The Problem Nobody Was Solving

Colombia's small restaurants and bars — often 5 to 30 employees — were managing shift scheduling on WhatsApp groups and running payroll on paper or Excel. Managers were spending hours each week reconciling hours, handling shift swaps through text messages, and manually calculating complex Colombian labor rules (overtime, night surcharges, Sunday premiums, "dominical" days).

The US had tools like 7Shifts and Homebase. But nothing existed for LATAM that handled local payroll regulations, Spanish-language UX, or the specific workflows of the Colombian restaurant industry. That gap was our starting point.

Chapter 2

Discovery: Getting Uncomfortably Close to the User

We ran structured user interviews with restaurant and bar managers across Bogotá, applying design thinking to uncover the real pain points — not just what users said they wanted, but how they actually managed shifts and payroll day to day.

What we found: managers didn't just need a scheduling tool. They needed an end-to-end system that connected shift data directly to payroll calculations, because the real pain was the hours spent reconciling what happened (actual clock-ins) with what was planned (the schedule) — and then translating that into correct Colombian payroll.

Real Artifact — Competitive Analysis (from Tips PRD)

Mapping the Competitive Landscape

We benchmarked against 7Shifts, Homebase, and local alternatives to identify what they got right and where the LATAM gap was. Key insight: US tools treated tips and payroll as separate systems. In Colombia, tips ("propinas") are deeply intertwined with compensation and must follow specific legal formulas. No existing tool handled this.

Chapter 3

From Wireframes to MVP in Under 3 Months

I owned the product from concept through wireframes to shipped MVP. We started with hand-drawn wireframes — literally sketching out every screen on grid paper — then iterated rapidly based on user feedback before writing any code.

Real Artifact — MVP Wireframes

Hand-Drawn UI Designs

Our early designs were sketched by hand on grid paper with detailed annotations. The payroll screen ("Hoja de Horas" — timesheet) connected scheduling data to payroll processing. Each wireframe included interaction notes, edge cases, and flow annotations — this was how we moved fast without losing fidelity on complex workflows like multi-step payroll processing.

Real Artifact — 38-Page MVP Requirements

Annotated Screenshot Specs

The MVP requirement document ran 38 pages of annotated screenshots — every screen captured with handwritten notes marking what needed to change, new features to add, and edge cases to handle. This was our living spec: fast, visual, and constantly updated as we learned from users.

The MVP shipped with three core modules: shift scheduling (create, assign, swap shifts), clock-in/out with geolocation verification, and a timesheet that fed directly into payroll calculations. We built on Bubble.io for speed, which let us ship in under 3 months.

Chapter 4

Payroll: The Hardest Problem We Solved

Colombian payroll is not simple math. Every employer does it slightly differently — overtime rules, night surcharges ("recargos nocturnos"), Sunday/holiday premiums, and tip distribution all follow complex legal formulas that vary by establishment.

Real Artifact — Payroll Design Flow

Multi-Step Payroll Processing

We designed a guided, multi-step payroll flow. Before the manager could finalize payroll, the system forced a shift review step — flagging shifts with missing clock-ins or clock-outs, and shifts where actual hours diverged significantly from scheduled hours. Then came the "Novedades Horarias" (hourly novelties) review, breaking down each employee's hours into ordinary, overtime, and night hours. Each column was editable, giving managers control while the system did the heavy lifting.

  • Step 1: Select payroll period and initiate
  • Step 2: Review flagged shifts (missing entries, >1hr discrepancies)
  • Step 3: Confirm hourly breakdowns per employee (ordinary, overtime, night)
  • Step 4: Apply adjustments and process
Real Artifact — Tips PRD

Tips Calculation Feature (Full PRD)

One of our most detailed product documents. The Tips PRD included problem alignment, value/risk assessment, competitive analysis against 7Shifts and Homebase, two detailed user narratives, explicit goals and non-goals, and key flow definitions. This is how we approached every major feature — structured problem framing before any design or engineering work.

  • Problem: Restaurants distribute tips differently (equal split, hours-based, role-based) with no standard tool
  • Goal: Automate tip calculation within the payroll flow using configurable rules
  • Non-goal: We explicitly scoped out tip reporting for tax purposes in v1
Chapter 5

Iterating Through Real User Friction

Shipping the MVP was just the beginning. We built structured feedback loops to capture exactly where users were getting stuck — and prioritized ruthlessly based on churn risk and activation impact.

Real Artifact — Implementation Friction Tracker

Onboarding & Activation Issues

We maintained a structured spreadsheet tracking every friction point in the implementation journey — from password creation to payroll configuration. Each item was categorized, rated by importance, documented with real cases, and paired with potential solutions. Examples:

  • Password reset links expired after one use — employees got stuck on their first login (Med/High priority)
  • Precise geolocation permissions were unintuitive — employees couldn't clock in, leading to frustration (High priority)
  • Payroll math configuration was confusing — every restaurant calculates "novedades" differently (High priority)
  • Adding users to multiple locations required navigating to Settings — not discoverable (Medium priority)
Real Artifact — Churn Post-Mortem

Structured Churn Analysis: The Amelia Case

When a trial customer churned, we ran a structured post-mortem. The Amelia case was a restaurant that signed up but never fully activated. Our analysis walked through the user's journey, identified the failure points, and critically asked: was this a mainstream churn signal (indicating a product gap we need to fix) or an edge case (a customer outside our ICP)?

Conclusion: the churn was driven by an edge-case mismatch — the restaurant had an unusual staffing model that didn't fit our scheduling paradigm. We documented it, extracted any actionable learnings, and moved on without over-rotating the roadmap. This disciplined approach to churn analysis kept us focused on building for our core user.

Chapter 6

Going Mobile: Task Management for the Floor

Restaurant managers don't sit at desks. Once the core scheduling and payroll product was stable, we extended into mobile-first task management — letting managers assign, track, and verify operational tasks (cleaning checklists, prep lists, opening/closing procedures) directly from their phones.

Real Artifact — Mobile Designs

Task Management Flows (Tareas Mobile)

We designed mobile task management with the same philosophy: structured, visual, and connected to the employee data we already had. Tasks could be assigned by role, tracked with completion status, and reviewed by managers — all from a mobile interface designed for the fast-paced restaurant floor.

Chapter 7

Go-to-Market: Cold Outreach in Colombia

We built our initial customer base through direct outreach — cold emails, WhatsApp messages, and in-person visits to restaurants in Bogotá. Our marketing was in Spanish, localized for the Colombian market, and focused on the specific pain points we'd validated in discovery.

Real Artifact — Cold Outreach Campaign

Mass Email & Direct Marketing

Our cold outreach templates were crafted around the specific pain points restaurant managers had shared in interviews — framed not as "here's our tool" but as "we know the problem you're solving with WhatsApp and Excel." Combined with referral incentives and local partnerships, this drove LTV/CAC above 4x.

Chapter 8

The Exit: Acquired by Bibisoft

Within 18 months, Cronos had grown to 1,300 paying users across top restaurants and bars in Colombia. We were offered $300K in seed financing — but instead accepted an acquisition offer from Bibisoft, a POS provider that saw Cronos as the missing workforce management layer for their restaurant clients.

The acquisition validated our thesis: purpose-built tools for LATAM's service industry, designed around local regulations and real workflows, could outperform localized versions of US products. The product I built continues to serve restaurants across LATAM.
